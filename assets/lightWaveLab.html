Introduction:

In this lab we used our newfound knowledge about waves, specifically light waves in order to find the speed of light using patterns of microwaves from a microwave. Our hypothesis was that the speed of light should be around 3 * 10^10 m/s, a widely accepted number.

Formulas Used:

    Speed of light = 2 x (distance between melted spots) x (frequency of microwave)

Error = |correct-yours|/correct * 100%

Materials:

     
  • Microwave
  • Microwave safe dish at least 5-6 inches across (bigger is better)
  • Mini-marshmallows
  • Chocolate Chips
  • Graham crackers
  • Ruler
  • Calculator

Procedure:

  1. Marshmallow & Chocolate Chip Trial:
    • If your microwave has a rotating platform, remove it.
    • Create a single, even layer of mini-marshmallows on a large, microwave-safe plate.
    • Microwave the plate for about 10 seconds. Adjust the time if needed; you want some marshmallows to be melted while others are not.
    • Carefully remove the plate. Using a ruler, measure the distance in centimeters between the centers of two adjacent melted spots. This distance is equal to half the wavelength of the microwaves.
    • To verify your results, repeat the entire procedure above using a layer of chocolate chips instead of marshmallows.
  2. Calculations:
    • Find the operating frequency (ƒ) of your microwave. This is usually printed on a sticker on the back or inside the door of the microwave, typically around 2450 MHz. Remember that 1 MHz = 106 Hz.
    • Calculate the speed of light (c) using your measurements with the following formula, where d is the distance between melted spots:
      Speed of light (c) = 2 × (distance in cm) × (frequency in Hz)
    • Compare your experimental value to the actual speed of light, which is approximately 3.00 × 1010 cm/s. Calculate your percent error to see how close you were.

Error Analysis:

   Our errors, while seemingly small looking at the numbers, were considerable, with around 50% error for one of our trials. The reason for this was likely the uncertain nature of our measurements, for instead of a precise singular point as a node, we had a big spot of meltiness, with little idea where to measure from. This could be fixed with a much, much larger microwave and a bigger surface area, that way we could measure across multiple nodes rather than just one. Another error could simply be the inconsistency of the ingredients themselves. It wasn't completely clear when the chocolate was melted, so a special material that could be placed as a single sheet, and then perhaps imaged with infrared after being microwaved would give us a better idea of exactly where the material is being heated up.

Conclusion:

Although the measurements did have error, they succeeded in giving a ballpark measurement of the speed of light. This is extremely useful to help increase the understanding of our universe, as light is literally the speed limit around which time and even space are defined. A real world application could be calculating communication delay between radio stations, or calculating and triangulating distances of radio stations given communication delays.
