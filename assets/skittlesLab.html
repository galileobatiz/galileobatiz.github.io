Purpose:

To simulate the concept of half-life through the transmutation of Carbon-14 (C-14) into Nitrogen-14 (N-14) using candy as a model for randomness and probability.

Theory:

146C is unstable due to having too many neutrons. One of the neutrons will change into a proton and produce a beta particle. 147N is produced.

146C → 147N + 0-1e + v

Theoretically, half the radioactive atoms will undergo nuclear decay in 5,730 years and change into N-14. In another 5,730 years, another 50% of the remaining atoms will change. The process continues until ALL the C-14 has changed into N-14.

Materials:

  • Skittles (representing atoms)
  • Cup
  • Excel (for graphing)
  • Paper towels

Procedure:

  1. Setup
    1. Obtain a cup for the skittles. We started with approximately 60 Skittles to ensure better statistical results.
    2. Your total number of skittles represents 0 half-lives (Time = 0), with 100% of the radioactive particles present.
    3. C-14 atoms are represented by the "S" side (Heads). N-14 atoms are the blank side (Tails).
  2. Trial 1
    1. Pour all the skittles onto a paper towel.
    2. Count and record the number of C-14 that remain (Heads). These are still radioactive.
    3. The Tails are the N-14 atoms created (decayed). Remove them from the population.
    4. Collect all the remaining C-14 (Heads) and place them back in the cup.
    5. Repeat this "shake and pour" process until all the C-14 has decayed into N-14.
  3. Trial 2 & Analysis
    1. Place all original skittles back into the cup. Switch jobs with your lab partner.
    2. Redo the lab a 2nd time, recording the C-14 remaining after each half-life.

Post Lab Questions & Discussion:

C-14 Dating Mathematics

Q: C-14 is used to date objects from the last 50,000 years. How many half-lives of C-14 take place in 50,000 years?

To find this, we divide the total time by the half-life of Carbon-14.
50,000 years / 5,730 years = 8.72 Half-Lives
(Note: In class we discussed the natural log formula relating to the decay constant where 6931.47 relates to mean lifetime calculations, but the raw half-life count is approx 8.7).

Decay Products

Q: C-14 dating is based on the nuclear decay of C-14. During C-14 decay, explain what is produced?

When C-14 decays, it turns into Nitrogen-14 (N-14). In this process, a neutron turns into a proton, ejecting an electron (beta particle) and an antineutrino.

Limitations of Carbon Dating

Q: Why is C-14 dating not used to date substances which are 12 years old or substances 1,000,000 yrs old?

Because dating relies on probability and measuring the change in ratio. For something only 12 years old, not enough C-14 has decayed to be measurably different from a modern sample. Conversely, for something 1,000,000 years old (which is nearly 175 half-lives), there is effectively zero C-14 left to measure. You would need an isotope with a much longer half-life, like Uranium-238.

Radio Tracers & Safety

Q: Why is it safe to take radio tracers?

Radio tracers used in medicine utilize isotopes with very short half-lives. This means they release their radiation quickly and then stabilize into non-dangerous isotopes. Because the half-life is short, the total time your body is exposed to the radiation is minimal, and the dosage is kept very low.

Waste Disposal

Q: "Low Level Radioactive Waste" (medical) vs "High-level Waste" (fission products). Why bury high-level waste deep underground?

Low-level waste (like from hospitals) decays quickly and is safe in shallow landfills. High-level waste, however, remains radioactive and generates heat for thousands or millions of years. We bury it deep underground for two main reasons:

1. Physical Isolation: It prevents humans or animals from accidentally interacting with it for geological timeframes.
2. Shielding: Radiation is a physical process. While air is a poor shield, hundreds of meters of dense rock and soil are excellent at blocking radiation. The Earth's crust naturally contains radioactive elements, so deep burial utilizes the Earth's natural shielding properties to protect the surface biosphere.

The "Element Z" Problem

Problem: Element Z occurs as Z-10 (99%) and Z-11 (1%). Z-11 is radioactive with a half-life of 100 years. If a sample has 12,000 total Z atoms and only 3 Z-11 atoms remain, how old is it?

Solution:

  • First, determine the original number of Z-11 atoms. If the sample has 12,000 atoms total, and 1% is normally Z-11, then: 12,000 × 0.01 = 120 original Z-11 atoms.
  • We started with 120 atoms and ended with 3.
  • Using the percentage remaining formula (or simply counting halves):
    • 120 → 60 (1 HL)
    • 60 → 30 (2 HL)
    • 30 → 15 (3 HL)
    • 15 → 7.5 (4 HL)
    • 7.5 → 3.75 (5 HL)
  • 3 remaining atoms is roughly 5.3 half-lives.
  • Age = 5.3 half-lives × 100 years/half-life = ~530 Years Old.
