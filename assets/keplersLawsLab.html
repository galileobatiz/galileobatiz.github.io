Kepler’s Laws:

The orbit of every planet in a two body system can be described mathematically by a secondary planet tracing the perimeter of an ellipse with the primary body as one of its foci.

The area of the path swept out during a consistent time interval of an orbit will not vary.

The square of the period of the orbit is directly proportional to the cube of its radius.

Formulas Used:

e = (distance between foci) / (major axis)
1/2 x (Dperihelion) x (vperihelion) x (time) = 1/2 x (Daphelion) x (vaphelion) x (time)
Aphelion or Perehelion = (SemiMajorAxis)(1 +/- Eccentricity)

Error Analysis:

Possible errors could include our inaccuracy when measuring the ellipses with a paper tube and some pencils poked through the table, our choice of days rather than a more precise measurement for the moons, and rounding. We could likely remedy these by following the procedure properly, taking care to draw multiple ellipses to average out errors; finding increasingly precise measurements for the periods and distances of Jupiter’s moons; and rounding less when doing math.

Conclusion:

While Kepler’s First Law was assumed, our findings served as evidence to support the Second and Third Laws. These are important to understanding the make of our solar system, and practical applications include helping to find more precise details about our own orbit/an earth year, as well as judging whether or not Pluto is a planet.
