Introduction:

[cite_start]

In this lab, we utilized real astronomical data to determine the rate of the universe's expansion, known as the Hubble Constant[cite: 46]. By measuring the redshifts and angular sizes of various spiral galaxies, we were able to calculate their velocities and distances. [cite_start]We used this data to estimate the age of the universe under two different models[cite: 52]. I hypothesize that the universe is really, really old.

Hubble's Law:

[cite_start]The recessional velocity of a galaxy is proportional to its distance from us[cite: 36]. This relationship is defined by the equation v = Ho × d.

Formulas Used:

v = c * z  (Velocity = Speed of Light * Redshift)
[cite_start]d = 22 kpc / angular_size (Distance using "Standard Ruler") [cite: 283]
v = H_0 * d (Hubble's Law)
[cite_start]Max Age = 1 / H_0 [cite: 312]
[cite_start]Age with Gravity = (2/3) * (1 / H_0) [cite: 323]

Procedure:

  1. Selection
      [cite_start]
    1. We examined 27 galaxy images and spectra, selecting only spiral galaxies with clear shapes and strong spectral lines[cite: 65, 162].
      2. [cite_start]
    2. We rejected elliptical galaxies because they lack the star formation regions needed for strong emission lines[cite: 156].
  2. Measurements
    1. Velocity: We measured the redshift of the Calcium K and H lines and the Hydrogen-alpha line. [cite_start]We calculated velocity using the Doppler formula v = c × z[cite: 193].
    2. Distance: We measured the angular diameter of the galaxies in milliradians. [cite_start]Assuming a "standard ruler" where all these spirals are approx 22 kpc across, we calculated distance[cite: 283].
  3. Analysis
    1. We plotted Velocity (y-axis) vs. Distance (x-axis).
      2. [cite_start]
    2. The slope of the best-fit line gave us the Hubble Constant (Ho)[cite: 298].
      3. [cite_start]
    3. We used Ho to calculate the age of the universe[cite: 312].

Questions & Error Analysis:

Measurement Challenges

[cite_start]

One major challenge was measuring the angular diameter. The galaxies are "fuzzy," making it difficult to decide where the edge of the galaxy ends and the background begins[cite: 253]. This introduces uncertainty into our distance variable. [cite_start]Furthermore, we measured along the longest axis because the galaxies are tilted; measuring the short axis would underestimate the size (and overestimate distance) due to the inclination angle[cite: 261].

Sources of Error

Our data fits the line well, but there are errors. NGC 3147 was a significant outlier with a much higher velocity-to-distance ratio than expected. This could be due to a poor distance measurement (the galaxy was noted as "weird spiral" in my notes) or peculiar velocity (motion not due to expansion). [cite_start]Additionally, the "Standard Ruler" assumption—that all spiral galaxies are exactly 22 kpc—is a generalization that naturally introduces error[cite: 88].

The "Pundits" Debate

[cite_start]

Historically, scientists argued whether the Hubble constant was 50 or 100 km/sec/Mpc[cite: 43]. Our result of 75 km/sec/Mpc falls squarely in the middle of this debate and aligns with modern consensus. I am ready to challenge the pundits of the past: the answer isn't 50 or 100, it's right in between.

Conclusion:

[cite_start]

This lab confirmed Hubble's Law: galaxies are receding from us, and the faster they move, the farther away they are.[cite: 36]. We successfully derived a Hubble Constant of approximately 75 km/sec/Mpc. [cite_start]Based on this expansion rate, we estimate the age of the universe to be between 8.5 and 12.7 billion years, depending on the effects of gravity[cite: 312, 323].
