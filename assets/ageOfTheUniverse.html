Introduction:

In this lab, we utilized real astronomical data to determine the rate of the universe's expansion, known as the Hubble Constant. By using calculated redshifts from spectrographic readings of galaxies' hydrogen and calcium content and their angular sizes as given by a website from U Washington, we were able to calculate their velocities and distances. We used this data to estimate the age of the universe. I hypothesize from my previous knowledge that the universe is 13.5 billion years old.

Hubble's Law:

The velocity of a galaxy away from us is proportional to its distance from us. This relationship is defined by the equation v = Ho × d.

Formulas Used:

v = c * z  (Velocity = Speed of Light * Redshift)
d = 22 kpc / angular_size (Distance assuming galaxy to by 22kpc)
v = H_0 * d (Hubble's Law)
Max Age = 1 / H_0

Materials:

  • Database of galaxy images and spectra
  • Digital formula for measuring galaxy angles
  • Digital application for managing data and forming lines of best fit
  • Computer

Procedure:

  1. Selection
    1. Use computer to open your database of galaxies and spectra, such as the Palmora Observatories readings in Washington University's website.
    2. From the 27 galaxy images and spectra, select only spiral galaxies with clear shapes and strong spectral lines whose wavelength can be determined
  2. Measurements
    1. Velocity: Find the redshift of the Calcium K and H lines and the Hydrogen-alpha lines by comparing the spectral lines of the regular elements with the recorded ones from distant galaxies, then calculate the velocity of those galaxies using the doppler formula v = c × z.
    2. Distance: Using your digital formula, measure the angular diameter of the galaxies in milliradians. Assuming a "standard ruler" where all these spirals are approx 22 kpc across, calculate the distance based off of that angle using basic trigonometry
  3. Analysis
    1. In your application for managin data plot Velocity (y-axis) vs. Distance (x-axis).
    2. The slope of the best-fit line gives the Hubble Constant (Ho).
    3. We used Ho to calculate the age of the universe.

Questions & Error Analysis:

Error Percentage

The accepted value of Hubble's Constant is 70.4 ± 3% = 68.3 to 72.5 km/sec/Mpc, given by Dr. Freedman's team at Uchicago.

Our measured value of 78.55 falls just outside this strict range. Calculating the specific percent error:
(|78.55 - 70.4| / 70.4) × 100 = 11.58%

Sources of Error + Measurement Challenges

One major challenge was measuring the distance using angles, because the galaxies are fuzzy around the edges, making it difficult to decide where the edge of the galaxy ends and the background begins and take measurements. This introduces uncertainty into our distance variable, on top of the already flawed assumption that every galaxy is exactly 22mpc across. Another notable source of error wwas my calculation for redshift/velocity, due to the subjective and decision based conclusion that many of the spectral graphs forced me to take. This judgement based conclusion is also a large factor in determining my Hubble's constant due to the fact that I selected which galaxies to graph in the first place, which could cause error as well, or even if it reached a more accepted number, would be more due to my decision than the objective data.
