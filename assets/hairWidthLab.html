Hair Width Lab

Miles, Gabriel, Koko, Tallie, Gali, Mia, Kiwi — October 2025

Introduction:

In this lab we used new knowledge about waves, specifically light waves, to determine the width of a hair in front of a laser by taking measurements of its interference pattern. We planned on measuring everyone’s hair, and my hypothesis was that Tallie’s hair would be the thickest.

Formulas Used:

Hair Width = (Wavelength of Laser × Distance to Wall) / (Distance Between Fringes × ½)

Materials:

  • Tape
  • Laser pointer
  • Batteries
  • A measuring tape (up to ~1 meter)
  • A precise measuring tool for 0-5 cm (calipers or a ruler)
  • Hair samples
  • A stable surface for the laser (e.g., a table)
  • Wall or screen
  • Calculator

Procedure:

  1. Find a table or flat surface approximately one meter away from a wall. This distance does not need to be precise, but should be measured.
  2. Pull out one of your or your groupmate’s hairs.
  3. Tape the hair vertically to the laser pointer's aperture so that it splits the beam.
  4. Place the laser pointer on the flat surface, aiming it perpendicularly towards the wall, and turn it on.
  5. Observe the horizontal diffraction pattern projected on the wall. It will consist of a bright central maximum with dimmer, smaller fringes on either side.
  6. Measure the full width of the large, central bright fringe.
  7. Using the formula, plug in your measurements. The width of the central fringe corresponds to twice the "distance between lines," so you can use half of this measurement in the denominator.
  8. Repeat steps 2-7 for each hair sample to determine everyone's hair thickness. The largest calculated value corresponds to the thickest hair.

Analysis: Tallie and Gali were tied for the thickest human hair, while Kiwi the horse had the overall thickest hair by far.

Error Analysis:

Because we never actually measured our hair’s breadth with another tool, it is unclear what our percent error was. However, several sources of error could include: inconsistent measurement of the distance from the laser to the wall, imprecise measurement of the central fringe width, or suboptimal placement of the hair on the laser. These issues could be mitigated with a dedicated mechanical apparatus that holds the hair at a fixed distance from the laser and has its own built-in screen for projection, allowing for more precise and repeatable measurements.

Conclusion:

Being able to measure the width of a hair just by looking at patterns of light demonstrates the practical power of wave optics. This principle of diffraction is quite useful. Imagine a planet passing in front of a star; the unique diffraction pattern could allow us to determine the size of that planet. A more local application is in calculating throw distances and screen sizes for film projectors to ensure a sharp image.
