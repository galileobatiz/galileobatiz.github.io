Introduction:

For our first lab we used a tissue box to determine the height of a staircase indirectly.
The staircase we measured is the outside staircase on the northeast face of Geffen Academy at Ucla
We held out the tissue box and dropped it off the staircase, using our knowledge of the acceleration of gravity here on earth
and the amount of time spent dropping in order to calculate the distance, using the kinematic equation known for displacement-time:
Δx = ut + (1/2)at^2 where
x is displacement
u is velocity at time initial(in this case 0)
a is acceleration(in this case is equal to g or the gravitational acceleration of earth, which is 9.81 m/s/s)
t is the time period in seconds
My hypothesis was that the building would be approximately 15 meters in height.

Procedure:

1. Visit the staircase on the northeastern face of Geffen Academy at Ucla, and have something or someone hold out a tissue box over the precipice of the highest accessible stair
2. Have a stopwatch used to time the period starting exactly when the tissue box is released to freefall down the height of the stairs, and end when the box hits the floor
3. Write down the time recorded
4. Get the tissue box back up the stairs to be dropped again, and repeat steps 1-3 10-20 more times.
5. Average the recorded times in order to lessen the effects of unpredictable variables
6. Using the resulting average as your time period, and your knowledge of gravitational accelleration, plug these numbers into the displacement time formula and solve for ∆x
7. ∆x is the height of your staircase

Data:

First drop: 1.5
2nd: 1.8
3rd: 1.3
4th: 1.5
5th: 1.6
6th: 1.6
7th: 1.7
8th: 1.4
9th: 1.7
10th: 1.6

The average of these numbers is their sum divided by the amount in the list, which in this case is about 1.57
1.57 becomes our time period, and we then plug that number into the displacement-time equation:

Δx = ut + (1/2)at^2

in this scenario, u(velocity initial) is zero, meaning the first part of the equation doesnt do anything
a is gravitational acceleration, or 9.81 m/s/s
t is our calculated period of 1.57
our equation then becomes
∆x = 0(1.6) + (1/2)(9.81)(1.57)^2
∆x ≈ 12 meters
our change in height, or just height of the staircase is 12 meters.

Materials:

- Method for recording time
- method for measuring time

Error Analysis:

We were likely unable to time the start of the stopwatch very accurately with the dropping of the box, neither were we likely able to
perfectly end the stopwatch along with when the box hit the floor. These could affect the times anywhere from 0.2 to 0.5 seconds based off of average human reaction time
Air resistance could also hypothetically slow the tissue box to a speed below gravitational acceleration. Another slight error by humanness is our inability to hold up the box out
over the precipice at a consistent height.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, our hypothesis that the building would be approximately 15 meters in height was correct, with a final measure of 12 meters.
This Lab is important in the larger context of the world because it demonstrates the ability of a physicist to measure things indirectly, which is important for
finding information about things we cannot access normally, like planets bajillions of miles away, or subatomic particles.
